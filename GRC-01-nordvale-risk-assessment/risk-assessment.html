Ten entries from the corporate risk register of a fictional UK-headquartered industrial and electronics distributor, assessed on a 5×5 impact × likelihood matrix, expressed as business impact, treated, mapped to NIST CSF 2.0 and ISO 27001 Annex A, and sequenced into a 30/90-day roadmap for a mixed technical and business committee.

01

Findings — Top of the Register

Every risk carries a business-impact statement: decision-makers fund consequences, not configurations. The two Criticals and the Severe lead the report.

R1 · CRITICAL 20 · I5 × L4

Unprotected cloud backup data

Backups in AWS are not locked or immutable. An account compromise could destroy the recovery path — days of order data, revenue and customer trust lost in a single ransomware event.

→ Immutability + MFA-delete + least privilege + restoration testing. Target residual: Minor.
R6 · CRITICAL 20 · I5 × L4

Government-related data in a public channel

Sensitive government-related data stored in a publicly accessible SharePoint channel — threatening contract termination, debarment and regulatory action. The most expensive event on the register.

→ Immediate restriction & relocation, exposure-window assessment, labelling + DLP. Target residual: Minor.
R2 · SEVERE 16 · I4 × L4

Outdated endpoint security agent on ~60% of devices

A majority of the endpoint estate runs a degraded security agent — the front door for ransomware and credential theft left half-guarded.

→ Forced updates, compliance dashboard, conditional access for non-compliant devices. Target residual: Minor.
IDENTITY CLUSTER · R3 R4 R5

One fix, four risks

Phishing failures, shared vendor accounts and hijackable sessions share a root cause: weak identity. MFA plus named accounts plus a joiner-mover-leaver process shrinks four register entries at once.

→ Highest-leverage control on the roadmap.

04

Treatment Roadmap

Sequenced by risk reduction per unit of effort, then dependency. The committee question this answers: what gets fixed in 30 days versus the quarter?

Next 30 days — fires & quick wins

  1. Restrict & relocate exposed data, assess exposure window (R6)
  2. Backup immutability + MFA-delete (R1)
  3. Reset all plaintext-exposed credentials; notify MSP in writing (R8)
  4. Force security-agent updates estate-wide (R2)
  5. MFA everywhere — cloud, portals, email (R1 R3 R4 R5)
  6. Secure/HttpOnly/SameSite cookies + HTTPS (R5)

Days 31–90 — programme actions

  1. Named vendor accounts + joiner-mover-leaver process (R4)
  2. Sensitivity labelling + DLP — public channels & AI uploads (R6 R10)
  3. AI acceptable-use policy + approved enterprise tool (R10)
  4. Warehouse network discovery, inventory, segmentation + NAC (R9)
  5. Patch-compliance dashboard + conditional access (R2)
  6. Supplier remediation: contractual clauses, pen test, insurance review (R7 R8)
  7. Backup restoration test + deletion alerting (R1)
Committee summary: two fires get put out this month for low effort. One identity fix shrinks four risks at once. The quarter's real work is visibility and governance — find the warehouse networks, label the data, and give AI a sanctioned path instead of a blocked one.
05

Peer Review — What Changed

The first draft went through peer review. The corrections are more instructive than the parts that were right first time.

  1. Rescored the phishing risk (Minor 9 → Significant 12). With 10% of a market entering credentials, likelihood is observed, not hypothetical — and privileged-account escalation keeps impact honest. Evidence moves scores.
  2. Fixed a scope error on shared accounts. Removed an out-of-scope patching control: shared credentials are an identity risk — accountability, audit, attribution. Every control must trace to that risk's causal chain.
  3. Governance over blocking for AI tools. Blocking alone drives shadow IT; the durable control is a sanctioned path — approved tool, acceptable-use policy, DLP guardrails.
  4. Contractual enforcement for supplier risks. A technical fix at a supplier doesn't stick without the SLA clause that makes it enforceable and repeatable.
  5. Business impact first, controls second. Slides tell the story; the register is the appendix. Decision-makers fund consequences, not configurations.
